Why a whole site about parking?

Because the trailhead lot — not the trail — is what ruins plans. Lots built for a quieter era meet Instagram-era demand, shoulders get patrolled, and "just park anywhere" turns into a citation or a tow. Elsewhere the lot isn't the answer at all: a shuttle system or a permit lottery is, and nobody explains how those interact with where you leave the car. Each guide here covers one trailhead with the numbers that matter: how many cars fit, when it fills, what goes on the dash, and the realistic plan B.

Coverage spans four Southern California ranges — the San Bernardinos (Lake Arrowhead–Crestline–Running Springs, Big Bear, Forest Falls), the San Gabriels (Chantry Flat, Switzer, East Fork, Icehouse Canyon), the San Jacintos (Idyllwild) and San Diego County (Cedar Creek Falls, Three Sisters, Mt. Woodson, Torrey Pines) — plus three Utah national parks: Zion, where a mandatory shuttle and the Angels Landing lottery decide everything; Bryce Canyon, where the shuttle is optional and the Visitor Center lot is posted 1-hour; and Arches, which dropped timed-entry reservations for 2026, leaving 409 published spaces at the three lots everyone drives to. Every fact is checked against official sources — Forest Service and Park Service pages, state and city parks, county sites, operators — and date-stamped, including the closures and seasonal rules that decide whether the drive is worth making at all.
